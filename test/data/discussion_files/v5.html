  • I'll get us started. Paul Kiel of ProPublica and I just completed an investigation of high-cost small-dollar cash-lending to military members and their families. We visited several big bases in Georgia, found the usual cluster of title lenders, installment lenders, pawn shops. We met and talked to military members and their families who were paying interest above the 36% APR cap mandated under the Military Lending Act. We found a lot of loans--like car title loans and online payday loans--are skirting the MLA by extending the loan term or other devices. So, the question I'd like to kick off with is: Is the Military Lending Act working? Has it made a difference?

  • One definition from a consumer law advocate I have interviewed: predatory loans are those that leave the borrower in a worse financial position after the loan than before. They are structured to extract so much money from the borrower, so that instead of being able to borrow, use the money, and pay back with interest and fees, they borrow, use the money, try to pay back, and end up deeper in debt and financial distress.

  • Re: Marketplace's question -- currently, it's illegal to offer an active-duty servicemember a payday loan (defined as having a term of 3 months or less) or an auto-title loan (of 6 months or less). But a payday loan of 4 months is fine, no matter how high the interest rate, and the same goes for an auto-title loan of 7 months. So the question facing the Pentagon now is whether to extend those terms. Because as we saw in Georgia and other states, it's really not hard to continue offering high-priced products that are not banned by the Military Lending Act.
